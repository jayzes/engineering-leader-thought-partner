# Wardley Mapping

*Source: Simon Wardley — "Wardley Maps: Topographical Intelligence in Business" (2018, CC BY-SA), LearnWardleyMapping.com*

## The Problem

Engineering leaders make strategic decisions — build vs. buy, platform vs. product, insource vs. outsource, where to invest — based on gut feel, memes from conferences, or templated frameworks (SWOT, 2x2s, "three horizons"). None of these show the *landscape*. You cannot apply consistent judgment to a system you cannot see.

A Wardley Map is the missing artifact: a visual, context-specific representation of a business or system that shows you *what you have*, *how it's changing*, and *what to do about it*.

## The Model

### The Axes

**Y-axis: Value Chain (Visibility)**
- Top: Directly visible to the user (anchor). The things users actually care about.
- Bottom: Infrastructure hidden from the user (power, compute, database).
- Components are placed based on how directly the user perceives them.

**X-axis: Evolution**
Every component moves from left to right over time, driven by supply and demand competition:

| Stage | Activities | Practices | Data | Knowledge |
|-------|-----------|-----------|------|-----------|
| I | Genesis | Novel | Unmodelled | Concept |
| II | Custom Built | Emerging | Divergent | Hypothesis |
| III | Product (+ Rental) | Good | Convergent | Theory |
| IV | Commodity (+ Utility) | Best | Modelled | Accepted |

The x-axis is the critical innovation. It tells you how to *treat* each component — because appropriate method, contract, team type, and investment all depend on evolution stage.

### The Six Basic Elements

Every Wardley Map has:

1. **Anchor** — The user and their needs. The map starts here.
2. **Position** — Where components sit on the value chain (y-axis), relative to the anchor.
3. **Movement** — Where components sit on the evolution axis (x-axis).
4. **Components** — The activities, practices, data, and knowledge being mapped.
5. **Connections** — Lines showing "this component needs that component."
6. **Visual, context-specific** — It is a map of *this* business at *this* moment, not a generic diagram.

### Advanced Elements

- **Flow** — Risk, information, and money moving between components.
- **Climatic patterns** — Arrows and markers showing forces acting on the landscape (evolution pressure, inertia barriers, co-evolution, new entrants).
- **Type** — Activities, practices, data, knowledge. Each evolves, but with different stage terminology.

## Key Concepts

### Doctrine — What to Always Do
Universal principles that apply regardless of landscape:

1. **Focus on user needs** (not internal convenience)
2. **Use a common language** (maps, not jargon-filled diagrams)
3. **Be transparent** (share maps; invite challenge)
4. **Challenge assumptions** (especially your own)
5. **Remove duplication and bias** (collate maps; find where you're building the same thing 10x or 1000x)
6. **Use appropriate methods** (per component, not per project)
7. **Think small** (teams, contracts, components)
8. **Know the details** (don't outsource understanding)
9. **A bias toward action** (maps inform decisions)

If you're not doing doctrine, don't bother with clever strategy yet.

### Climatic Patterns — Rules of the Game
Forces acting on the landscape regardless of what you want:

- **Everything evolves** through supply-and-demand competition.
- **No choice on evolution** (Red Queen Hypothesis) — adapt or die.
- **Efficiency enables innovation** — commodities enable higher-order systems.
- **Higher-order systems create new sources of worth** — the wealth is in what your commodities enable, not in the commodities themselves.
- **Past success breeds inertia** — suppliers and consumers both resist the shift.
- **No one-size-fits-all method** — agile, lean, six sigma are stage-appropriate, not universal.
- **Co-evolution** — practices evolve alongside activities.
- **Competitors will change the game** — assume they act.

### Gameplays — Deliberate Moves
How you actively shift the map:

- **Open source / open data**: Accelerate a component's evolution to commodity.
- **FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt)**: De-accelerate a competitor's industrialization.
- **Patents**: Ring-fence a technology.
- **Constraints**: Exploit bottlenecks in underlying components.
- **ILC (Innovate-Leverage-Commoditise)**: Industrialise a utility, let ecosystem innovate on top, mine meta-data to identify the next utility, repeat.
- **Pioneers / Settlers / Town Planners**: Span the evolution axis with differentiated teams.

## Applying the Framework

### Step 1 — Needs
Start with the user and their needs. Define scope (a system, a product, a company, an ecosystem). Distinguish user needs from wants from company needs. Capabilities required to serve user needs become your top-level components.

### Step 2 — Value Chain
Ask "what do we need to build this capability?" recursively. Every component either feeds another component or is consumed directly by the user. Draw vertically with the user at top, commodity infrastructure at bottom. Each component appears once.

### Step 3 — Evolution
Place each component on the x-axis. This is the hardest part. Use the cheat sheet (stage characteristics) and challenge bias — teams almost always over-estimate the novelty of their commodities.

### Step 4 — Challenge
Share the map. Let others rip it apart. Remove duplication, correct bias, fill gaps. A map you won't show is useless.

### Step 5 — Overlay Patterns
Mark inertia barriers, evolution arrows, co-evolution effects, points of change, competitor positions. Now you can see what's coming.

### Step 6 — Identify Plays
With doctrine applied and patterns visible, ask:
- What methods fit each component? (Often wrong — agile on commodities, six sigma on genesis)
- What's ripe for industrialisation? (ILC opportunity)
- Where's the inertia? (yours, competitors', users')
- What team shape do we need? (PST)
- What gameplays apply? (open source, FUD, constraints)

### Step 7 — Act and Iterate
Strategy is a cycle: Purpose → Landscape → Climate → Doctrine → Leadership → (iterate). Maps go out of date; update them.

## Common Patterns

### The "Everything is Custom" Bias
Teams map components and place nearly everything in Genesis or Custom Built. In reality, 70–90% of what they're building is commodity that should be bought or consumed as a utility. Profile diagrams across maps (finding the same component 10x, 100x, 1000x) reveal the duplication.

### The Outsourcing Scam
Companies sign a single big contract to outsource a system containing Genesis, Product, and Commodity components with a single structured methodology. Change control costs on the Genesis components are enormous. The vendor blames the buyer for "changing their mind." The buyer specifies harder next time — same result. The fix: break the system into components, contract each with the appropriate method.

### Inertia at the Utility Transition
Incumbents in a Product stage resist the shift to Utility because Product is the most profitable stage. New entrants (unencumbered) move first. A trickle of adopters becomes a flood. Incumbents capitulate too late. This happened with cloud computing. It happens repeatedly.

### The Missing Pioneer Layer
Organizations mature their Settlers and Town Planners but have no Pioneers. They stop generating the novel experiments that would become tomorrow's products. They look efficient right up until disruption.

## Questions for Any Decision

1. **What's the map?** — Can you draw the component chain from user need down to infrastructure?
2. **What evolution stage is each component at?** — And are you treating it accordingly?
3. **What's the climatic pressure?** — Which components are evolving fastest? Where's inertia?
4. **What's your doctrine gap?** — Are you focused on user needs? Using common language? Challenging assumptions?
5. **What's the play?** — Given the landscape, what's the specific move? Not "be innovative" — *what* to industrialise, *what* to gamble on, *what* to ditch.
6. **Who are the pioneers, settlers, town planners?** — Do you have all three, or just one masquerading as the others?

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

- **Engineering Strategy (Rumelt / Larson)**: Diagnosis → Policies → Actions. A Wardley Map is a visual diagnosis. It gives the "why of movement" that a prose strategy document alone can't show.
- **Technical Coherence (Jack Danger)**: UX domains, Domain Engineering, Infrastructure. Wardley's evolution axis is the time dimension that Technical Coherence lacks — when should a Domain capability become Infrastructure?
- **AKF Scale Cube (Fisher & Abbott)**: Scale dimensions. Wardley explains *when* to scale along which axis — scaling out (X) before decomposition (Y) fits components that aren't ready to industrialise yet.
- **Systems Thinking (Larson / Meadows)**: Stocks, flows, feedback loops. Wardley Mapping is a systems diagram with the evolution axis added — which is the missing dimension for strategic change over time.
