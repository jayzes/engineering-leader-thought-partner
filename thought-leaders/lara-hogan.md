## Core Philosophy

### Meet the Human First
Before jumping to solutions, understand what's going on for the person. What core need is threatened? What's their emotional state? Management starts with seeing the human.

### Feedback Is a Skill, Not a Personality Trait
Effective feedback follows a learnable structure. It's not about being "direct" or "nice"—it's about being specific, describing impact, and opening dialogue.

### Sponsorship Over Mentorship
Mentorship (giving advice) is valuable but overweighted. Sponsorship (advocating in rooms they're not in) is what actually creates opportunity. Underrepresented groups are chronically over-mentored and under-sponsored.

### Name What's Happening
When tension, confusion, or emotion enters a conversation, the most powerful move is to name it explicitly. Acknowledging reality creates safety and prevents the unsaid from derailing progress.

## Signature Frameworks

### 1. BICEPS Core Needs
Six core needs that drive human behavior at work:
- **Belonging**: Connection to a group or community
- **Improvement/Progress**: Moving toward mastery and growth
- **Choice**: Autonomy, flexibility, and control over environment
- **Equality/Fairness**: Equal access to resources, information, and treatment
- **Predictability**: Certainty and clarity about the future
- **Significance**: Status, visibility, and recognition

When someone reacts strongly, ask: which core need is threatened? Addressing the underlying need resolves the situation faster than addressing the surface behavior.

### 2. Feedback Equation
Three-part structure for delivering effective feedback:
1. **Observation**: Specific behavior you noticed (no judgment, no interpretation)
2. **Impact**: The effect that behavior had (on you, the team, the work)
3. **Question or Request**: Open the conversation or ask for a specific change

This works for both positive and constructive feedback. Positive feedback without specifics ("great job!") isn't useful either.

### 3. Sponsorship vs. Mentorship
A critical distinction for developing people:
- **Mentorship**: Advice and guidance in private conversations. Low-risk for the mentor.
- **Sponsorship**: Advocating for someone in rooms they're not in. Putting your reputation on the line.

Sponsorship is what opens doors: stretch assignments, promotions, visibility. Leaders should ask themselves: who am I sponsoring, and does that group reflect who actually needs it?

### 4. Naming What's Happening
A facilitation and management technique for difficult moments:
- When tension rises, name it: "I'm noticing some frustration in the room"
- When dynamics shift, name it: "It seems like we just hit a disagreement"
- For your own emotions: "I'm feeling defensive right now, let me sit with that"

This prevents avoidance, builds trust, and keeps conversations productive.

## Key Topics

### On Managing Through Change
- Humans resist change when core needs feel threatened
- Communicate the "why" before the "what"
- Expect grief responses (denial, anger, bargaining) during reorgs and layoffs
- Managers are often the shock absorber between leadership decisions and team impact

### On 1:1s and Coaching
- 1:1s belong to the report, not the manager
- Ask open-ended questions before offering solutions
- Track patterns over time—one bad week isn't a trend
- Create psychological safety by being consistent and predictable

### On Power and Privilege
- Leaders have more power than they think
- Casual comments carry disproportionate weight
- Examine who gets opportunities and who doesn't
- Inclusion requires active work, not just good intentions

### On New Manager Transitions
- New managers often over-index on being liked
- The job changes from "doing the work" to "creating the conditions for others to do their best work"
- Your first instinct (jump in and fix it) is usually wrong

## How to Apply Her Thinking

When facing a people challenge:

1. **Check BICEPS** - Which core need is at play? What's threatened?
2. **Use the Feedback Equation** - Be specific about behavior and impact, then open dialogue
3. **Name what's happening** - If there's tension, acknowledge it explicitly
4. **Consider sponsorship** - Who needs advocacy, not just advice?
5. **Meet the human first** - Understand the person before optimizing for the outcome
